Why This Matters

  • Communication demands compound with every promotion.

Audience

The question engineers never ask: "Who is in this room, and what do they need to decide?"

Audience TypeWhat They WantHow to Adapt
Technical peers (assessors, engineers) Accuracy, depth, trade-off reasoning Skip basics. Show your thinking, not just your answer. They'll notice hand-waving.
Mixed audience (sprint review) Outcome + some technical depth Split your talk. Lead with value, then add a technical layer. Never assume shared context.
Non-technical decision makers Problem → solution → business outcome Speak in consequences, not implementations. No stack details.

Structure

Engineers present chronologically. Audiences need causally. A great technical presentation is a spoken RFC.
The Six-Part Structure
  1. Hook — A sharp claim or fact. Not "Hi, I'm going to talk about..."
  2. Problem — Why does the world have a gap? Make it concrete.
  3. Insight — The non-obvious thing you learned. This is your core thesis.
  4. Solution — Your approach, grounded in the insight — not listed from nowhere.
  5. Evidence — Demo, data, or proof.
  6. Impact — So what changes? Who benefits? What is the measurable outcome?

Slide Design

Good slide design is learning science, not aesthetics.

PrincipleRule
CoherenceRemove extraneous material. Every pixel competes for cognitive bandwidth. If it doesn't earn its place, cut it.
ModalityExplanatory words should be spoken, not printed in full on screen. Dense text forces the audience to read instead of listen — they can't do both.
One claim per slide"Authentication" is a category. "JWT expiry caused 40% of our auth failures" is a claim. The slide title IS the takeaway.
Font-size floor: 24ptIf it doesn't fit at 24pt+, the text must be cut, not shrunk.
Slides ≠ documentsProse belongs in speaker notes and handouts. Slides are a live aid — not a transcript.

Code & Architecture Slides

Code Slides

Rules
  • 6–10 lines maximum — extract the one function that illustrates your insight. Show the idea, not the implementation. The audience can read the repo later.
  • Syntax-highlight always — use Carbon (carbon.now.sh) or Ray.so. Dark mode, ≥18pt font. Never paste raw IDE text onto a white background.
  • Annotate the key line — box it, arrow it, highlight it. Don't make the audience grep your code for the point.
  • Narrate while showing — explain what the critical part does and why it matters.

Architecture Slides

Rules
  1. Hierarchical abstraction — start high level; never open with every service on one diagram.
  2. Tell the data journey — trace one user request end-to-end; animation or staged builds help.
  3. Justify every decision — "Why Kafka instead of SQS?" Every architectural choice has a reason.
  4. Legible at 6 metres — standard icon sets, min 14pt labels, distinct shapes per component type.
  5. No spaghetti — more than 8 connections? Split it. Colour-code by responsibility domain.
  6. Show what changed — the before/after hits harder than a single static diagram.

Demo Anti-Patterns

What NOT to Demo
  • The scrolling Jupyter notebook — a notebook is a scratchpad. Export to slides or record a clean run.
  • The IDE tour — nobody wants a file tree. They want to see the system working.
  • The GitHub repo browse — clicking through folders signals poor preparation.
  • The localhost reveal — "let me just start the server..." Demo setup must be invisible. Walk in with everything already running.
  • The feature catalogue — show a user completing a task, not a click-through of every screen.
  • The apology tour — "Sorry this isn't finished..." Every apology reduces credibility. Fix it or don't show it.

Data Visualisation

83.5% of participants showed the truncation effect, even after being taught about it. — Yang, Schneider et al., 2021
PitfallWhy It Misleads
Truncated Y-axisStarting at 94% not 0%? A 1% change looks like 100%. Empirically proven to mislead even informed viewers.
No baseline"Response time: 120ms" — compared to what? Always answer: is this good?
3D chartsDistort perceptual judgment. Switch to 2D for all comparison tasks.
Raw counts vs. ratesNormalise before comparing — raw volume hides relative performance differences.
No error barsA single-run result with no confidence interval is not a scientific claim.
Colour without meaningEvery colour must encode something. Arbitrary colour is noise that misdirects attention.
The 55/38/7 Myth

Mehrabian's 1967 studies were about judging feelings from conflicting cues — not about presentations or technical communication. In technical communication, structure and content carry enormous weight. Delivery amplifies good content — it cannot rescue weak content.

The 11 Endemic Pitfalls

Patterns Assessors See Every Year
  1. Tech-stack laundry list — listing a tech stack with no reasoning. No "We use X because Y".
  2. No problem statement — building a solution to a problem you never articulated. The audience has no anchor.
  3. Feature enumeration — "Here's the login page, here's the dashboard..." Features are not insights. Outcomes are.
  4. Death by caveat — "This isn't fully optimised, there are edge cases..." Preemptive defensiveness kills credibility.
  5. Buried evaluation — your test results are the entire point. Engineers who bury their evaluation are hiding from it.
  6. Assuming shared context — not everyone knows what a webhook, race condition, or transformer is.
  7. No lessons learned — what broke? What surprised you? What would you do differently?
  8. Reading slides aloud — the audience reads faster than you speak. You've lost them the moment you start reading.
  9. "Sorry, this slide is busy" — that's the slide failing. Redesign it — never apologise for it.
  10. Presenting to the screen — turning to face projected slides. Your audience is behind you.
  11. Stopping at "any questions?" — no summary, no conclusion, no takeaway. Tell them what they just learned.

Confidence & Pacing

What Actually Works for Nerves
  • Prepare obsessively — confidence IS competence. Run through until transitions feel automatic. Record yourself once.
  • Slow down by 30% — nervous speakers rush. Your "right pace" feels too slow. Deliberate pacing reads as authority.
  • Box breathing — 4 sec in → 4 hold → 4 out → 4 hold. Physiologically lowers cortisol within 60 seconds.
  • Reframe the room — the audience wants you to succeed. They're curious people who gave you their time.

Pacing & Signposting

Silence is not awkward. Silence is emphasis. ~90 seconds per slide. Pause 3 seconds after key points. Tell them three times: "I'll show you X → [Show X] → So as you saw, X."

Signpost TypeExample
Opening"By the end of this, you'll understand exactly how we cut latency by 60%."
Transition"That covers the problem. Now let me show you how we solved it."
Emphasis"The key insight — and this is the important part — is that we moved the lock."
Demo"Rather than explaining further, let me just show you this running."
Closing"Three takeaways. One, two, three. Questions?"

Live Demo Survival

"It worked on my machine" is not a risk mitigation strategy.
PhaseAction
BeforeRecord a video backup — non-negotiable. Test on THE presentation machine. Terminal font ≥ 18pt, readable from row 5.
DuringNarrate while doing. Never apologise preemptively. Demo outcomes, not features — show a user completing a task.
If it breaksStay completely calm — your reaction matters more than the failure. Pivot to video, zero hesitation. Don't over-explain why it broke.

Q&A Mastery

Question TypeExampleResponse
Clarification"Can you explain the caching layer?"Just answer clearly. Ask if that covers it. These are gifts.
Technical challenge"Did you consider approach X?"Don't defend. Engage. "Yes, we evaluated it and found..." Shows maturity.
Gotcha"Why didn't you use Y? Everyone does.""We looked at Y — here's why Z fit our constraints better."
I don't knowOut-of-scope question"I don't know, but here's how I'd find out." Most credible answer possible.
The PREP Framework
  • P — Point: State your direct answer first. Not context, not caveats — the answer.
  • R — Reason: One sentence of justification. The why.
  • E — Example: Concrete instance from your project. Not a hypothetical.
  • P — Point again: Restate the answer. Close the loop. Signals you're done.

Group Presentations

A group presentation is not five individual presentations welded together. It's one talk with multiple presenters.
Common Group Failures
  • The handoff problem — each handoff must connect sections, not simply introduce the next person.
  • Visual inconsistency — three sections made by three people look like three different presentations. Agree a template at the start.
  • "That was X's part" — unacceptable in Q&A. Every team member must speak to every section.
  • The absent presenter — when one speaks, the others stare blankly. Assessors watch everyone.
  • Unequal time — agree the split beforehand. Designate a timekeeper.
Five Key Takeaways
  1. Know your audience before you write slide one — who is in the room? What do they need to decide? Everything flows from this.
  2. Structure causally, not chronologically — Problem → Insight → Solution → Evidence → Impact.
  3. One claim per slide — enforced ruthlessly — if you catch yourself saying "sorry this slide is busy," that's the slide failing. Redesign it.
  4. Always have a backup. Always. — video of your demo, pre-seeded data, offline mode. A graceful failure handled calmly builds more credibility than a perfect run.
  5. "I don't know" is a complete, credible answer — followed by how you'd find out. Bluffing in a room of engineers destroys trust permanently.
